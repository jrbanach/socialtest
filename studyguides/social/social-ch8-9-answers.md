## Vocabulary (20 terms)

1. **Minutemen** — Colonial soldiers who could be ready to fight in a minute
2. **Loyalist** — A colonist who stayed loyal to Britain during the American Revolution
3. **Patriot** — A colonist who wanted the American colonies to be independent from Britain
4. **Petition** — A written request signed by many people to ask for a change
5. **Preamble** — The introduction to a document that explains its purpose
6. **Mercenary** — A soldier who is paid to fight for a country other than their own
7. **Blockade** — When ships or soldiers stop people or supplies from coming in or out of a place
8. **Inflation** — When prices go up and money buys less than it did before
9. **Revolution** — A major change, often the overthrow of one government to create a new one
10. **Desert (verb)** — To leave the army without permission and not come back
11. **Grievances** — Complaints or reasons people are unhappy and want things to change
12. **Tyranny** — Cruel or unfair control by a ruler or government
13. **Independence** — Freedom from control by another country or ruler
14. **Proclamation** — An official public announcement
15. **Delegate** — A person chosen to represent others at a meeting or assembly
16. **Congress** — A group of representatives who meet to make laws or decisions
17. **Militia** — A group of ordinary people who train to fight in emergencies
18. **Neutral** — Not taking sides in a conflict or argument
19. **Quarter (verb)** — To provide soldiers with a place to stay and food
20. **Repeal** — To cancel or take back a law

## Short Answer Questions (20 questions)

### 1. Why did the British march to Lexington?
The British marched to Lexington on April 19, 1775, to arrest colonial leaders Samuel Adams and John Hancock, and to seize weapons and gunpowder the colonists had stored in Concord.

### 2. What was the effect of British taxation on the colonies as a whole?
British taxation (like the Stamp Act and Townshend Acts) made the colonists very angry because they had no say in Parliament — "no taxation without representation." These taxes united the colonies and led to protests, boycotts, and eventually violent conflicts.

### 3. How did the Boston Tea Party express the views of many colonists?
Colonists dressed as Native Americans and threw 342 chests of British tea into Boston Harbor in 1773. This was a protest against the Tea Act and British taxes, showing that colonists strongly opposed British control and "taxation without representation."

### 4. How did the Daughters of Liberty support the boycott of British goods?
The Daughters of Liberty were a group of women who made homemade cloth (homespun) and other goods so colonists wouldn't need to buy British products. They organized spinning bees and encouraged others to buy American-made items.

### 5. What does the Declaration of Independence say about Britain?
The Declaration of Independence lists many complaints (grievances) against King George III. It says Britain treated the colonies unfairly — taking away rights, taxing without consent, and keeping soldiers in the colonies. It declares the colonies should be free and independent.

### 6. Describe the Intolerable Acts
The Intolerable Acts (1774) were strict laws Britain passed to punish Boston for the Tea Party. They closed Boston Harbor, forced colonists to quarter (house) British soldiers, and took away much of Massachusetts' self-government. These acts united the colonies against Britain.

### 7. How did Washington force the British out of Boston?
George Washington had cannons hauled from Fort Ticonderoga and placed them on Dorchester Heights overlooking Boston in March 1776. The cannons threatened British ships and troops, making it too dangerous for them to stay. The British evacuated Boston.

### 8. Who was Prince Hall and what was he known for?
Prince Hall was a free Black man in Boston during the Revolution. He petitioned for the rights of African Americans and helped found the first African American Masonic Lodge. He spoke out for education and freedom for Black people.

### 9. Why was signing the Declaration of Independence a dangerous act?
Signing the Declaration was dangerous because the signers were committing treason against Britain. If caught, they could lose their property, be thrown in prison, or be executed. They were risking their lives for independence.

### 10. What actions did the Sons of Liberty take in response to the Stamp Act?
The Sons of Liberty organized public protests, burned stamps, destroyed the homes and offices of tax collectors, and led boycotts of British goods. They used these actions to show Britain that colonists would not accept unfair taxes.

### 11. Describe the Boston Massacre
The Boston Massacre happened on March 5, 1770, when a group of colonists confronted British soldiers. The soldiers fired into the crowd, killing five colonists including Crispus Attucks. Patriots used this event as propaganda to build anger against British rule.

### 12. How did the Committee of Correspondence communicate?
The Committees of Correspondence communicated by writing and sending letters between the colonies. These letters kept colonists informed about British actions and helped them organize a united response.

### 13. What were the 2 goals of the Tea Act?
1. To help the struggling British East India Company sell its extra tea in the colonies
2. To get the colonists to accept Parliament's right to tax them by keeping a tax on tea

### 14. How was the TIMING an important strategy in Washington's surprise attack on Trenton?
Washington crossed the Delaware River on Christmas night (December 25, 1776) and attacked early the next morning. The Hessian soldiers were not expecting a fight after their holiday celebrations. The timing meant the enemy was tired and unprepared, giving the Americans a major victory when morale was very low.

### 15. How did women keep the revolution alive?
Women contributed to the Revolution by making uniforms, blankets, and ammunition. They cooked food, cared for wounded soldiers, ran farms and businesses while men were away fighting, spread important news, and raised money for the Continental Army.

### 16. Mary Ludwig Hays and Deborah Sampson
**Mary Ludwig Hays ("Molly Pitcher")** brought water to soldiers during battle and took over firing her husband's cannon when he was wounded at the Battle of Monmouth. **Deborah Sampson** disguised herself as a man named "Robert Shurtleff" and enlisted as a soldier. She fought in several battles before her secret was discovered.

### 17. What did King George III do with the Olive Branch Petition?
King George III refused to read the Olive Branch Petition. He rejected the colonists' request for peace and instead declared that the colonies were in open rebellion. He then sent more soldiers to fight them.

### 18. Who wrote the Declaration of Independence?
Thomas Jefferson was the main author of the Declaration of Independence. He was chosen by the Continental Congress to write it. A committee including John Adams and Benjamin Franklin helped revise the document before it was adopted on July 4, 1776.
