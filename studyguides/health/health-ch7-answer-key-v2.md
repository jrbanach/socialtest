## Knowledge Topics (6 items)

### 1. Example of Drug Abuse
**Answer:** Examples of drug abuse include sniffing glue, chewing tobacco, and taking steroids to build muscles. Drug abuse is when someone uses an illegal drug OR uses a legal drug in a harmful way on purpose. It is different from drug misuse, which is accidental.
**Parker's original kept?** ⚠️ Replaced — Parker said "using an illegal drug in harmful ways" but didn't give specific examples and missed the "on purpose" distinction.

### 2. BAC (Blood Alcohol Concentration)
**Answer:** The amount of alcohol in a person's blood. When a person drinks alcohol, it goes directly into the bloodstream from the stomach and small intestine. BAC depends on three things: (1) how much alcohol a person drinks, (2) how fast the person drinks, and (3) the person's body size.
**Parker's original kept?** ✅ Yes — Parker's core definition was correct. Extra detail added from textbook for completeness.

### 3. Generic Drugs VS Brand Name Drugs
**Answer:** Generic drugs have no brand names but have the same main ingredients as brand-name drugs. Generic drugs are often cheaper than drugs with brand names.
**Parker's original kept?** ⚠️ Updated — Parker said "same thing with different name." Kept the idea but added the key point: generic drugs are often cheaper.

### 4. Toxins in Tobacco Smoke
**Answer:** The three main toxins in tobacco smoke are:
- **Nicotine** — a stimulant drug that speeds up the heartbeat and affects the brain; very addictive
- **Tar** — a thick, gummy substance that sticks to the linings of the airways and can kill the cells that line them
- **Carbon Monoxide** — a poisonous gas that takes the place of some oxygen in the blood, making it hard for blood to carry oxygen to body cells
**Parker's original kept?** ✅ Yes — Parker named all three correctly. Descriptions added from textbook for completeness.

### 5. Withdrawal Symptoms
**Answer:** People going through withdrawal may shake or feel nauseous. They may see and hear things that aren't there. The symptoms can be very serious and can even cause death.
**Parker's original kept?** ✅ Yes — Parker's answer matched the textbook symptoms.

### 6. Nar-Anon and Al-Anon
**Answer:** Nar-Anon and Al-Anon are programs that help people who are close to people who have addictions — including family members and friends of all ages. Counselors help them deal with the anger and pain they feel and support a person who has decided to quit. **Alateen** is a separate recovery program specifically for teens who are close to people with addictions.
**Parker's original kept?** ⚠️ Updated — Parker said "for teens" but that's actually Alateen. Nar-Anon/Al-Anon are for anyone. Corrected the distinction.

## Short-Answer Topics (3 items)

### 1. Why Do People Abuse Drugs?
**Answer:** The textbook lists six reasons why young people abuse drugs:
1. **Family difficulties** — not feeling loved or supported by family, feeling empty
2. **Drug abuse in the family** — seeing adults they trust do it and believing it is okay
3. **Peer pressure** — believing they need to use drugs to fit in with peers
4. **Negative self-concept** — not thinking highly of themselves, feeling awkward or having a negative body image
5. **Depression** — wanting to escape sad feelings that don't go away
6. **Media influence** — being influenced by tobacco and alcohol ads instead of focusing on the harmful effects
**Parker's original kept?** ⚠️ Updated — Parker mentioned peer pressure and family (good instincts), but the textbook expects all six reasons. Kept his concepts, expanded to full list.

### 2. Reading/Following Instructions with Medicine
**Answer:** Important safety rules for using medicines:
1. Take medicine only from your parents or guardian, or another adult they approve of
2. Follow the instructions that come with your medicine — take the right dose
3. Tell your doctor and pharmacist about any other medicines you take
4. Take only your own medicines — other people's medicines may be too strong or not right for you
5. Stop taking the medicine and tell your parents or guardian right away if you have side effects
6. Keep medicines in their containers — the containers have instructions for safe use; don't use after the expiration date
7. Check the seal on OTC medicine packages — do not buy if the seal is broken
**Parker's original kept?** ⚠️ Updated — Parker had rules 1, 2, and 3 correct. Added rules 4–7 from textbook.

### 3. Resisting Drugs
**Answer:** Ways to resist pressure to use drugs:
1. **Say no** loudly and firmly
2. **Give your reasons** for saying no — wanting good grades, following parents' guidelines, wanting to control your actions, obeying laws, reaching your goals in life
3. **Walk away** from the person pressuring you
4. **Match your behavior to your words** — show that you mean it
5. **Ask an adult for help** if you need it
6. **Be proud of yourself** when you say no — you are respecting yourself and making a responsible decision
**Parker's original kept?** ⚠️ Updated — Parker had "say no" and "walk away" (good). Changed "make an excuse" to "give your reasons" per textbook. Added "ask an adult for help" and other strategies.
