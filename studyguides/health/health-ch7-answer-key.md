## Vocabulary Definitions

**Definition:** The unpleasant reaction experienced when a drug is no longer taken. People going through withdrawal may shake or feel nauseous. They may see and hear things that aren't there. The symptoms can be very serious and can even cause death.

## Knowledge Topics

### 1. Example of Drug Abuse
**Answer:** The textbook gives these examples of drug abuse: sniffing glue, chewing tobacco, and taking steroids to build muscles. Drug abuse is when someone uses an illegal drug OR uses a legal drug in a harmful way on purpose. It is different from drug misuse, which is accidental. For example, if someone is supposed to take two pills and takes four by mistake, that is drug misuse — but if someone uses legal drugs in unsafe ways on purpose because they like how the drugs make them feel, that is drug abuse.
**Source:** Pages D8, D25

### 2. BAC (Blood Alcohol Concentration)
**Answer:** Blood alcohol concentration (BAC) is the amount of alcohol in a person's blood. When a person drinks alcohol, it goes directly into the bloodstream from the stomach and small intestine. BAC depends on three things: (1) how much alcohol a person drinks, (2) how fast the person drinks, and (3) the person's body size. A 12-ounce can of beer, a 5-ounce glass of wine, and a 1.5-ounce glass of liquor each contain about the same amount of alcohol.
**Source:** Page D11

### 3. Generic Drugs VS Brand Name Drugs
**Answer:** Many drugs have brand names — companies think brand names will make people want to buy their drugs. Generic drugs have no brand names but have the same main ingredients as brand-name drugs. Generic drugs are often cheaper than drugs with brand names. A generic drug and a brand-name drug may do the same thing.
**Source:** Page D5

### 4. Toxins in Tobacco Smoke
**Answer:** There are many harmful substances in tobacco and tobacco smoke. The three main toxins are:
- **Nicotine** — a stimulant drug found in tobacco that speeds up the heartbeat and affects the brain. Nicotine is very addictive.
- **Tar** — a thick, gummy substance found in tobacco smoke. Tar sticks to the linings of the airways. The airways make mucus to try to get rid of the tar. Tar can kill the cells that line the airways.
- **Carbon Monoxide** — a poisonous gas found in tobacco smoke. Carbon monoxide takes the place of some of the oxygen in the blood. This makes it hard for the blood to carry oxygen to body cells.

The textbook also mentions **smokeless tobacco**, which is tobacco that is chewed or placed between the cheek and gums. High levels of nicotine enter the body through the gums when smokeless tobacco is used.
**Source:** Page D17

### 5. Withdrawal Symptoms
**Answer:** People who have addictions and then stop using drugs may go through withdrawal. During withdrawal, people may shake or feel nauseous. They may see and hear things that aren't there. The symptoms of withdrawal can be very serious and can even cause death. For heroin specifically, people who try to quit may have pain, upset stomachs, and movements they can't control. They may need medicine to help them handle the withdrawal symptoms. Sometimes people use prescription medicines to help with the physical effects of withdrawal.
**Source:** Pages D25, D28, D34

### 6. Nar-Anon and Al-Anon
**Answer:** Nar-Anon and Al-Anon are programs that help people who are close to people who have addictions (such as family members and friends). Living with a person who abuses drugs or alcohol can be hard — the person may say and do hurtful things. Counselors can help family members deal with the anger and pain they feel. They may also help family members support a person who has decided to quit. There is also a program called **Alateen**, which is a recovery program just for teens who are close to people who have addictions.
**Source:** Page D35

## Short-Answer Topics

### 1. Why Do People Abuse Drugs?
**Answer:** The textbook lists several reasons why young people abuse drugs:
- **Family Difficulties** — Young people who don't feel loved and supported by their family may feel empty. They might abuse drugs to change the way they feel.
- **Drug Abuse in the Family** — When adult family members abuse drugs, young people might do the same thing because they see adults they trust doing it and believe it is okay.
- **Peer Pressure** — Young people might believe that they need to abuse drugs to fit in with their peers. The pressure to feel accepted can feel overwhelming.
- **Negative Self-Concept** — Some young people do not think highly of themselves. They might feel awkward or have a negative body image. They are at a higher risk for abusing amphetamines, steroids, or other drugs.
- **Depression** — Some young people have depression that does not go away. They want to escape their sad feelings and might abuse drugs to change their mood.
- **Media Influence** — Companies that make tobacco and alcohol products spend millions of dollars on ads. Some young people are influenced by these ads instead of focusing on the harmful effects.

**Source:** Pages D31–D32

### 2. Reading/Following Instructions with Medicine
**Answer:** The textbook gives these important safety rules for using medicines:
- **Take medicine only from your parents or guardian** or another adult they approve of.
- **Follow the instructions** that come with your medicine. Take the right dose (the amount of medicine you should take). The label may tell you how to take the medicine — you may need to take it with food or drink lots of water.
- **Tell your doctor and pharmacist** about any medicines you take, because sometimes drugs can change the action of other drugs when taken together.
- **Take only your own medicines**, not someone else's — other people's medicines may be too strong or not the right medicine for you.
- **Stop taking the medicine and tell your parents or guardian right away** if you have any side effects (unwanted reactions to a drug).
- **Keep medicines in their containers** because the containers have instructions for using the medicines safely. Don't use the medicine after the expiration date.
- **Check the seal** on packages of OTC medicine. Do not buy OTC medicine if the seal is broken.

**Source:** Pages D6–D7

### 3. Resisting Drugs
**Answer:** Being drug free means choosing not to abuse drugs. The textbook describes several ways to resist pressure to use drugs:
- **Say no** when someone pressures you to abuse drugs. Say it loudly and firmly.
- **Give your reasons** for saying no (good reasons include: wanting good grades, wanting to follow parents' guidelines, wanting to control your actions, wanting to obey laws, wanting to reach your goals in life).
- **Walk away** from the person pressuring you.
- **Match your behavior to your words** — show that you mean it.
- **Ask an adult for help** if you need it.
- **Be proud of yourself** when you say no to drug abuse — you are respecting yourself and making a responsible decision.

There are also laws to protect people: It is illegal for minors to buy or use alcohol, and illegal for people under 18 to buy tobacco products. Three U.S. Government departments (the FDA, DEA, and ATF) make policies and enforce laws about drugs.

**Source:** Pages D37–D39
